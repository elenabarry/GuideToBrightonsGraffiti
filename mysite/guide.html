About

Gallery of Graffiti

Brighton’s street art is known to rival Bristol’s. This Guide will take you through the best pieces Brighton has to offer.

The artwork is close together which means that you can walk around the pieces like you would do in a gallery, while also enjoying Brighton’s fresh sea air and bohemian vibes.

Powerful Messages

Brighton’s graffiti has powerful messages behind it, from women and the LGBT community, to making fun of our social media obsessed culture.

Share your discoveries

If you find any masterpieces that I have missed out, please feel free to share your discovery.

Kensington Street

Kensington Street has a high concentration of street art murals by local and international street artists, including: Odisy, Aroe and Sinna One. These paintings have been fenced off for their own protection.

Trafalgar Lane

Trafalgar Lane is similar to the Berlin Wall as the whole surface of the wall is covered in artwork. This wall is a 'paint with permission' wall which means the work on the wall in constantly changing. If you are lucky you might catch a glimpse of street artists at work.

Trafalgar Street

Right at the top of this street, you will find the Prince Albert pub, which has a giant tribute to passed musicians. It is also the original location of Banksy’s 2004 ‘Kissing Policemen’, one of his most famous pieces.

Providence & Elder Place

Behind the London Road shops, Providence and Elder Place are a treasure trove of raw and plentiful street art.

Don't stick to the program!

New street art pops up every day in Brighton. You will find it in the most bizarre places, so don't be afraid to venture forth.

Artists

Banksy

Banksy is a famous anonymous graffiti artist from Bristol. His work is known for delivering a satirical political message (BBC, 2017). A replica of Banksy’s ‘Kissing Policemen', replaced the original at the Prince Albert pub in Trafalgar Street. The original was sold to an anonymous buyer in Miami for £345,000. The Original was sold to an anonymous buyer in Miami for £345,000 (The Guardian, 2014). nother replica can also be found outside the Astoria theatre in Gloucester Place.

Aroe

“There’s always been a progressive and productive scene here. Brighton is now known as a place for pushing and inventing new stuff.” Aroe in an interview with the Brighton Source (Kendall, 2012).Aroe turned the previously drab Kensington Street into a colourful back alley. He is part of 'MSK', one of the best known graffiti crews in the world (Kendall, 2012). Together with Odisy he created the Run DMC mural which features a giant chessboard.

Sinna One

This mural of dogs was painted in Kensington Street by Sinna One. It was created for the Brighton Festival and was inspired by the 2016 guest director, Laurie Anderson's love of dogs (One, 2016).

Fat Heat

Fat Heat is a Hungarian graffiti artist and illustrator, who describes his passion for street art as an 'addiction' (Heat, 2014). His blog presents his inside and outside pieces.

Odisy

Odisy is a critically acclaimed London graffiti artist, who transformed the exterior of the tattoo shop in North Road with the ‘Blue Dragon Tattoo’ (Visitbrighton.com, 2017).

Cassette Lord

Cassette Lord is a graffiti artist originally from Portsmouth. He moved to Brighton to run the 'Artscape' project which is about teaching and creating graffiti art on walls and murals to young offenders as community service (Dreyfus, 2010). His work has a retro freestyle approach. You can find Cassette Lord's tag in multiple places around the city, especially on telephone exchange boxes which have been transformed into bright cassette boxes.

Join In

Free Walls

There are many 'Free Walls' in Brighton that the city council have made legal to graffiti on, these include, Kings Place Car Park, the Black Rock, and the fencing of Whitehawk Community Centre. If you would like to find out which walls are free in your area, you can use Legal Walls to find out. Otherwise, it is illegal to graffiti on any surface without the owner's permission, and the city council will remove any graffiti on public property.

Adopt a green box Scheme

Brighton and Hove city council encourages community art projects with their ‘adopt a green box’ scheme (Brighton-hove.gov.uk, 2017). The idea is to paint murals onto exchange boxes as a way of deterring anti-social behaviour and further graffiti.

The council is partnered with both Virgin Media and the Youth Offending Service. They provide the paint and brushes for anyone who would like to adopt a green exchange box. There are currently 200 boxes adopted across the city.

If you are interested in getting involved fill in a report form.
